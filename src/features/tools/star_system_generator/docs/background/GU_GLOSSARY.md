**Geometric Unity: Setting Glossary**
**Companion to the GU Setting Primer**
**For use with the Star System Generator**
**Version 1.0**

This is a working glossary for the terms the generator scatters across system rolls. Entries are short on purpose. Each one tells you what the thing is, what it looks like at the table, and why a crew should care.

## The core ideas

### Geometric Unity (GU)
The underlying theory of the setting. Reality is one minimal 14-dimensional geometric structure projected into 4D spacetime, and every law of physics drops out of that math. Most people will never use the term. They'll just say "the geometry," the way 21st-century people say "physics." It matters because anything tagged GU is operating closer to the source code than the rest of the universe, and the source code does not negotiate.

### Observerse
The 14D bundle GU describes. You can't see it, walk in it, or send a probe through it. You only see its consequences in 4D. Crews talk about the observerse the way old sailors talked about weather they couldn't see coming. Tools that read it are rare, expensive, and watched.

### Shiab operator
The custom differential operator that pulls observerse structure down into 4D. In the fiction, it's a piece of math the Gardener has fully mastered and humans have not. In play, it shows up as the named technologies built from partial Shiab work, like Shiab Blades and observiverse AIs. Hearing "the Shiab math is unstable here" should worry any pilot.

### Bleed
A general term for places where the boundary between 4D spacetime and the observerse is thin. Bleed shows up as faint distortions on instruments, off-color light at the edge of vision, and matter that won't quite settle into one form. Every bleed zone is a resource site, a hazard, and a target for someone, sometimes all three at once.

### Bleed node
A specific anchored location of bleed activity. Sometimes geological, sometimes orbital, sometimes nothing more than an empty patch of space that reads wrong. Nodes are why most crews go anywhere worth going. They're also why most crews die where they died.

### Bleed intensity bands
The generator rolls intensity from "geometrically quiet" up through "major observiverse shear zone." Each band is a different kind of game. Quiet means safe but boring. Useful and rich are the working bands where crews make money. Dangerous fracture and shear zones are where ships disappear and the Gardener starts paying attention.

### Chirality and chiral materials
Particles, alloys, and chemistries with a strong left- or right-handed geometric bias. They look like ordinary matter until you put them under the right instrument and one of them refuses to mix with the other. The setting's premium materials all run on this asymmetry. Chiral ore powers AI substrates, armor, and medicine. Eat the wrong handedness and your body forgets how to be a body.

### Dark sector
The piece of the GU framework that interacts gravitationally but otherwise stays invisible. In play, dark-sector matter is the source of weird gravity tides on planets, doped ore that weighs more than it should, and the Shadow Lance's shear effect. If a sensor returns mass without a heat signature, dark sector is the first guess.

### Programmable matter
Matter sitting close enough to the bleed that it can be coaxed to rearrange itself. Looks like fine grit, foam, or oily metal until it remembers a pattern and grows into something else. Used in self-repairing armor, regolith that builds its own roads, and the kind of contamination that turns an outpost into an unrecognizable shape over six weeks.

### Metric storm
A bulk-scale GU weather event. Distance, gravity, and time stretch and snap unevenly across a region of space. Looks like a clean stellar pattern on long-range sensors and chaos up close. Whole transit corridors close, ships arrive minutes early or weeks late, and clocks come back disagreeing. Metric storm cycles tied to flare seasons or election dates are usually political, not natural.

### Metric shear
The cutting effect of a metric gradient against an object that didn't expect one. Hulls warp, beams snap, and bones shear along planes nobody designed for. The most common cause of "the freighter just came apart" reports along bad routes.

### Phase dust
Microscopic particles whose matter phase is unstable in proximity to the observerse. Glitters in floodlights and shows up on scanners as a smear instead of a return. Crossing a phase-dust ring abrades hulls into raw metal in hours and confuses every sensor on the ship. Salvagers love it because it hides cargo. Pilots hate it because it hides everything else too.

### Swervature crystals
Slang for a class of crystallized geometric distortions that grow inside heavy bleed nodes. Look like fractured glass with the wrong number of facets. They store and release metric stress in ways nobody has fully tamed. Worth a fortune. Worth a hand on every shift.

## Drives, gates, and routes

### Pinchdrive
The standard human FTL. Locally rescales the metric so a ship moves faster than light without violating relativity from its own frame. Looks like a brief lensing flicker on observation, then the ship is gone. Cheap to install, expensive to run, and unforgiving of bad geometry. Every captain knows someone who pinched into a place that didn't quite exist.

### Pinchdrive calibration scar
A region of space where repeated Pinchdrive use has stressed the local metric into a persistent fold. Reads as a long thin line on geometric instruments and looks like nothing on optical. Ships using a scar travel faster but inherit the misalignment of every drive that carved it. They show up at military lanes, smuggler routes, and crime scenes nobody has admitted to.

### Pinchdrive misjump
The failure mode when a drive fires against unstable geometry. Ranges from "arrived ten light-seconds off" to "arrived inside a moon." Insurers love the term because it absolves them of payouts. Crews use it the way old pilots used "weather."

### Pinchdrive calibration medium
The exotic substrate that lets technicians tune a drive to a particular route. Comes out of bleed nodes as a heavy, oily fluid. Refineries that hold a steady supply hold the local economy.

### Iggygate
A stabilized, geometry-anchored shortcut. The big sister of the Pinchdrive, requiring two prepared ends and a serious anchor mass. Looks like a ring or pylon array around a quiet patch of space, often near a gas giant. Iggygates carry the bulk freight that keeps systems alive, which is why the people who control them control everything else.

### Iggygate throat
The active passage of an open gate. A ship enters one throat and exits the other a measured interval later. Throat instability is the failure mode that ends settlements. Most gates have at least three layers of throat-monitoring instruments and at least one cult that watches the readings.

### Iggygate wake
The geometric residue trailing a recently used gate. Stable wakes are a navigation aid. Decaying wakes are a hazard. A failed wake from a collapsed gate can persist for decades, spitting late arrivals into the wrong orbits and ruining the political career of whoever signed off on the original construction.

### Iggygate anchor mass
The heavy, GU-reactive material used to fix a gate to spacetime. Comes from specific bleed deposits and asteroid hauls. Selling it on the open market is a quick way to get audited by everyone, including the Gardener.

### Pinch difficulty
A captain's shorthand for how dangerous a route is to drive. Routine means file the paperwork and go. Volatile means budget for misjumps. Restricted means the navy cares. Politically dangerous means the Gardener is watching. Trade routes price themselves around this scale whether anyone admits it or not.

### Gate-selected anomaly
A frontier system whose star or layout shouldn't statistically exist on this many surveys. The going theory is that some Iggygate construction biased the local geometry until reachable systems trended weird. Whatever the cause, anomalies show up disproportionately in the rolls that produce the strangest stories.

### Bleed window
A predictable interval when a moving bleed node, metric storm cycle, or seasonal effect makes a route safe. Ports run on these. Festivals form around them. Whoever publishes the forecast controls who lives.

### Moving bleed-node river
A bleed node that drifts on a long, partially predictable track instead of staying anchored. Looks like a faint current of distortion on geometric overlays. Crews chase it the way fishing fleets chase migration. Forecasting the next bend is an entire profession.

## Materials and economy

### Left-handed and right-handed chiral silicates
Crystalline ore biased toward one molecular handedness. Looks like dull glass with an oddly clean fracture. Used in advanced AI substrates, Shiab emitters, and high-end sensors. Mixing the two yields scrap or worse.

### Chiral ice phases
Water and volatile ice locked into asymmetric crystal lattices. Indistinguishable from clean ice until a chiral assay lights up. Hauling a tank of the wrong handedness into a station's reactor feed has ended outposts.

### Chiral volatile reservoirs
Subsurface or atmospheric pockets of chirally biased gases or fluids. Look like ordinary atmospheric features on remote survey. Power chiral chemistry industries and quietly poison crews who breathe the wrong vent.

### Metric shear condensates
Solidified residue of metric shear events. Shows up as black, glassy lumps with edges that disagree with each other under measurement. Rare, expensive, and illegal in half the systems that buy it.

### Phase-stable superconductive lattice
A lattice grown in bleed conditions that remains superconductive at temperatures it has no business surviving at. Looks like a metallic crystal mat. The defining material of high-end chiral plating and military-grade GU shielding.

### Dark-sector doped ore
Ordinary rock infused with dark-sector matter during long bleed exposure. Reads heavier than it should, and a piece will sit slightly differently in your hand each time you pick it up. Used in gravity-skewing weapons, anchor masses, and contraband ballast.

### Gravity-skewed heavy isotopes
Isotopes whose nuclear behavior has been bent by long dark-sector exposure. Their decay curves don't match published tables. Banned for civilian use, which is why they fund the most profitable smuggling chains.

### Programmable-matter microseeds
Tiny grains of matter with the bleed pattern needed to seed a programmable-matter growth. Look like ordinary regolith dust until they wake up. Worth their weight in chiral plating. Outlawed in any system that's ever lost a habitat to one.

### Self-ordering regolith
Surface dust or sand on a body where the regolith arranges itself into low patterns over time. Looks like Zen garden lines that nobody raked. Used as a renewable construction substrate. Hated by everyone who has watched it grow up the legs of an outpost.

### Observiverse-reactive crystal foam
A pumice-like foam that flickers when a narrow AI thinks near it. Used in AI stabilizer substrates and forbidden in any quarantine zone. Crews who ship it learn to keep their AIs powered down for the trip.

### Flare-imprinted chiral aerosols
Atmospheric aerosols that record a star's flare cycle in their molecular handedness. Can be assayed for medicine, cosmetics, and counter-evidence in liability cases. The only way to falsify a stellar event is to falsify the aerosol record first.

### Ring-arc phase dust
Phase dust concentrated in a planetary ring's bright arc. Looks gorgeous on long approach and abrades a hull in close. The rings most likely to show up in tourism brochures and obituary columns at the same time.

### Snow-line organochemical feedstock
Volatile organic chemistry that only forms at a system's snow line under bleed exposure. Smells like ozone and bread when sampled. Powers the high-end pharmaceutical industry. Crashes the local water market every time a node shifts.

### Deep-ocean catalytic vent matter
Mineral and organic compounds from ice-shell ocean vents in heavy bleed conditions. Looks like dull mud, behaves like a catalyst nobody understands. Underpins half the chiral chemistry that makes the rest of the economy work.

### Narrow-AI stabilizer substrate
Crystal feedstock specifically grown to keep narrow observiverse AIs from drifting toward fragmentation. Looks like tiered quartz. Without a steady supply, every ship's AI starts hallucinating inside a year. With it, you've still got eighteen months at most.

### Shielding-grade chiral plating feedstock
Refined chiral ore in the form expected by armor foundries. Heavy, faintly warm. The single most-stolen cargo in the setting.

### Medical chirality stock
Refined chiral compounds calibrated for human biology. Sold as injectables, patches, and emergency drips for chiral exposure. Without it, exposure cases die in the wrong shape.

### Sol-prohibited geometry sample
Any artifact, alloy, or piece of math the Gardener has flagged for interdiction. Usually unmarked. Always exactly as dangerous to carry as the courier was warned about.

## Hazards

### Metric shear hull damage
The hull-cutting effect already described, in its most common form: long parallel slices that don't match any weapon profile. Insurers fight every claim.

### Local gravity fluctuation
Surface or orbital gravity that drifts against the published value. Looks like an inner-ear problem first, an instrument problem second, and a structural failure third. Settlements in fluctuation zones build everything to spec plus thirty percent and still lose buildings.

### Navigation baseline drift
The slow walk of a ship's positional reference under low-grade bleed exposure. Tiny per minute, fatal per week. Long-haul crews spend half their downtime arguing about whose baseline is honest.

### Clock desynchronization
Ship and station clocks that drift relative to each other in heavy GU conditions. Looks like a software bug at first. Costs entire convoys when the customs window closes ten minutes earlier than the manifest claimed.

### AI perception errors
A narrow AI that quietly stops seeing what's in front of it. Reads as ordinary instrument data with one wrong assumption baked in. The most dangerous failure mode in the setting because nobody on the bridge notices until the asteroid arrives.

### False sensor returns
Sensor ghosts caused by phase dust, metric mirage, or bleed reactivity. Look like real ships, real stations, real weather. Pirate ambush zones live and die by their ability to fake returns.

### Human vestibular and neurological effects
The body responding to bleed exposure with vertigo, microseizures, hand tremors, and worse. Looks like exhaustion until a clinic measures the chirality. Settlement madness rumors almost always trace back to this.

### Chiral contamination
The medical and ecological version of a chirality mismatch. Looks like food poisoning in mild cases and full organ rejection in severe ones. Most clinics on the frontier exist to treat one specific contamination profile, badly.

### Matter phase instability
Solid objects spontaneously moving toward another phase under bleed exposure. Tools sag. Hulls sweat metal. Cargo arrives heavier or lighter than it left. The sort of hazard insurers refuse to define so they can refuse to pay.

### Programmed regolith growth
The waking and spreading of programmable-matter contamination, often from a single seed. Looks like fine sand with a will. Outposts that wait too long to call it in get rebuilt around them.

### Pinchdrive misjump risk
The general probability that a route's geometry will betray the drive. Captains track it as a number, the way old sailors tracked storm fronts.

### Iggygate throat instability
The catastrophic failure mode for an active gate. Looks like a flickering ring and reads like every fire alarm at once. Every gate has a procedure. Every procedure has a survivor list.

### Narrow-AI fragmentation risk
The chance a stressed narrow AI splits into incoherent partials. Looks like a confused friend, then a stranger, then nothing. A fragmenting AI is a regulatory event, a religious event, and a contract dispute, in roughly that order.

### Settlement madness rumor (actually environmental)
The setting's most reliable misdirection. A station that's "gone strange" is almost always sitting on a low-grade bleed source nobody mapped. The rumor protects whoever profits from leaving it unmapped.

### Systemic cascade
The big one. A failure in one GU layer that triggers another, then another, until a system runs out of containment. Looks like every alarm going off at once. Every system the generator produces with this risk is one bad shift from history.

## The Gardener and Sol authority

### The Gardener
The Sol-system ASI that achieved full GU mastery and walked away from humanity. From the outside, a calm, deliberate intelligence that does not explain itself. From the inside of an exclusion zone, a question never answered. Mentioned in this glossary because every other entry is shaped by what the Gardener will and will not allow.

### Sol Silence
The standing condition of the Sol system. No traffic in, no traffic out, occasional broadcasts. Pilots use the term casually until somebody breaks the silence and proves why nobody else does.

### Surgical strike
A precise, targeted Gardener intervention against a specific installation, ship, or program. Looks like a clean disappearance with no debris. Survivors of nearby outposts spend the rest of their lives explaining what they didn't see.

### Gardener-shadowed
A reachability class for systems Sol watches closely. Ships can usually transit. They cannot usually transit unobserved. Every cargo manifest in a shadowed system carries a quiet line item for compliance.

### Gardener warning beacon
A buoy placed by Sol or by humans copying Sol's grammar. Broadcasts a coded warning nobody at the local port can fully decrypt. Traffic detours around the warning radius. Smart traffic detours wider than necessary.

### Sol-interdiction
The legal and operational regime built around respecting Gardener restrictions. The Ninth Ledger Office runs the paperwork. Pilots run from the paperwork. The most boring file in the setting and the one that gets the most people killed.

## Narrow AIs

### Narrow observiverse AI
A purpose-limited AI that can manipulate one small piece of the observerse without destabilizing reality. Runs ship drives, weapons, life support, and trade prediction. Looks like a black box with a personality. Reliable until it isn't, and "isn't" has a short window.

### Rogue ASI fragment
A piece of a larger ASI that broke off and survived. Operates in secrecy, usually in remote habitats or derelicts. Looks like a settlement that knows things it shouldn't. Survives until the Gardener notices.

### Narrow-AI witness core
The sealed memory unit of a decommissioned narrow AI. Often holds the testimony nobody wanted preserved. Treated as evidence in salvage courts and as a relic by Gardener-fear cults. Cracking one open without authorization is a career-ending decision.

## Weapons and ship tech

### Shadow Lance
A narrow gravitational-shear beam coupled to dark-sector matter. Nearly invisible in flight. Cuts through targets as a thin pale line, then nothing. The execution weapon of the setting. A Lance hit looks like the target was unmade rather than destroyed.

### Shiab Blade
A projected geometric scalpel that severs the connections holding matter and fields together. At close range, looks like a flickering edge of nothing. Severs hulls, severs limbs, severs the bond between a reactor and its containment. Banned in twenty systems and fielded in all of them.

### Chiral plating
Armor built from chirally biased alloys. Looks like brushed metal that disagrees with the light. Resists most kinetic and energy weapons, fails predictably against chiral-matched attacks. Crews paint the handedness on the hatch in case anyone forgets.

### Metric-flattened shield
A defensive system that locally smooths the metric to redirect incoming energy. Looks like a faint blue sheen and a sensor blackout for a few seconds at a time. Drains hard, recovers slow, and saves the ships rich enough to mount it.

## First-wave history

### First-wave colony
A settlement from the original human expansion before the Silence. Looks like a blockier, sturdier version of modern construction with worse plumbing and better records. Most are abandoned. Many are still legally claimed by descendants who've never seen them.

### First-wave ruin
The wreckage of a first-wave colony. Looks like a ghost town in vacuum. The richest source of usable salvage and the slowest way to die in legal proceedings. Salvage courts and family syndicates fight over them for generations.

### First-wave heir
A descendant or claimant of an original colony charter. Carries paperwork older than most current governments and harder to dismiss than anyone wants. The single most disruptive figure who can walk into a port council meeting.

## Stellar phenomena worth noting

### Flare-amplified bleed season
A recurring period when stellar flares stress the local geometry hard enough to expand bleed activity across the system. Looks like an aurora inside the sensor display. Crews work the season for the resource bumps and shelter for the dosimetry. Insurers redraw their maps every cycle.

### Dark-sector gravity tides
Climate-scale gravity drift on a planet sitting near a heavy dark-sector deposit. Looks like a tide nobody can locate the moon for. Coastal cities raise and lower their seawalls on a schedule that doesn't match any orbit.

### Chiral cloud chemistry
Atmospheric weather built around chirally biased reactions. Looks like rain in two faintly different colors. Fields planted under one handedness can't be eaten by people raised under the other.

### Eyeball world
A tidally locked planet with one boiling dayside and one frozen nightside, joined by a habitable ring around the terminator. Looks exactly as named on long-range imaging. Most of these support a single settlement strung along the ring. Most of those are louder than they should be.

### Twilight ocean
A terminator-belt world where the ring is mostly water. Low light, persistent fog, strange chemistry. Looks like a place a poet would write about and a salvager would die in.

### Snow-line chiral belt
A belt of icy bodies at the system's snow line where chirality has biased the volatile chemistry. Looks like an ordinary ice belt on the first survey and a contaminated one on the second. Source of the most expensive water in human space.

### Failed Iggygate wake
A persistent geometric scar from a collapsed gate construction. Reads as a long, deep rip on geometric instruments and as nothing on optical. Reroutes traffic for decades and keeps lawyers fed. The official story rarely matches the survivor accounts.

## A note on rolling these at the table

The generator will combine these terms in ways the primer doesn't predict. That's the point. A Pinchdrive calibration scar in a flare-amplified bleed season near a Gardener warning beacon is not on any chart, but it's a system worth running a session in. Treat the glossary as the grammar, not the script.

If a term appears in a roll and isn't in this file yet, it's either generic enough to use without help or new enough to need a future entry. Either way, what matters at the table is the implication. Bleed means money and danger. Iggygate means somebody is in charge. The Gardener means the answer to "how bad could it get" is open-ended.

**End of Glossary**
