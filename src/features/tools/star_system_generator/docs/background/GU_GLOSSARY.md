**Geometric Unity: Setting Glossary**
**Companion to the GU Setting Primer**
**For use with the Star System Generator**
**Version 2.1**

This is a working glossary for the terms the generator scatters across system rolls. Thirteen hero terms cover the setting; the variants the generator produces fold under their parent. Read the entry, scan the *Appears as* line, then check *At the table* for play hooks — you'll know what you're looking at when an unfamiliar phrase shows up in a roll, and you'll have a session seed to spin off it.

## The geometry

### Geometric Unity (GU) and the observerse
Geometric Unity is the underlying theory of the setting: reality is one minimal 14-dimensional geometric structure projected into 4D spacetime, and every law of physics drops out of that math. The observerse is the bundle GU describes — you can't see it, walk in it, or send a probe through it, only watch its consequences land in 4D. Most people will never use either word; they'll just say "the geometry," the way 21st-century people said "physics." It matters because every other entry in this glossary is shaped by what the Gardener will and will not allow, and the tools that read the observerse directly are rare, expensive, and watched.

*At the table:* "Geometry-literate" is what core-world adjusters call a witness who has to die — captains who learned the math the wrong way wear their licenses on a chain so the next crew can claim the policy. A retired observerse mathematician offers cheap geometry-reads off live sensor takes; half rate, twice the heat from anyone watching for civilians with that kind of tool. A theorist's notebook encodes coordinates for a *physical* observerse access site — the syndicate that killed her husband wants them too.

### Bleed
A general term for places where the boundary between 4D spacetime and the observerse goes thin. Bleed shows up as faint distortions on instruments, off-color light at the edge of vision, and matter that won't quite settle into one form. Every bleed zone is a resource site, a hazard, and a target for someone — sometimes all three at once. Crews talk about bleed the way old sailors talked about weather they couldn't see coming.

*Appears as:* anchored **nodes** (the working sites where most crews go and most crews die), timed **windows** (predictable safe intervals — ports run on these), seasonal **blooms** under stellar flares, drifting **rivers** that crews chase like fishing fleets, and dead **scars** left by collapsed gates. Bleed **intensity bands** scale from quiet through useful and rich to major shear zones; long exposure hits crews as vertigo, microseizures, and tremors. Stations rumored to have "gone strange" almost always sit on an unmapped low-grade source.

*At the table:* A bloom predicted Tuesday opens Saturday, and the harvester fleet that pre-positioned is now adrift inside it; the pirate clan that bribed the meteorologist is inbound on a quiet vector. A drifting river the harvesters call the Long Brown is two weeks late at its bend, and the freelance pilot who chased it found a fully intact first-wave refinery riding inside — got out alive, once. A station nineteen months "gone strange" has stopped answering, and the auditor sent to map the seep beneath has not checked in.

### Chirality
Particles, alloys, and chemistries with a strong left- or right-handed geometric bias. Looks like ordinary matter until you put it under the right instrument and one handedness refuses to mix with the other. The setting's premium materials all run on this asymmetry — chiral plating, chiral medicine, chiral substrates inside every narrow AI. Eat the wrong handedness and your body forgets how to be a body.

*Appears as:* **ore and silicates** (mined, refined into plating and AI substrates), **ice and volatiles** (water and gases locked into asymmetric lattices, identical to clean ice until a chiral assay lights up), **aerosols** (atmospheric, recording stellar flare history), chiral **plating** (brushed-metal armor that disagrees with the light — crews paint the handedness on the hatch so nobody mixes a load), **medical stock** (calibrated for human biology — without it, exposure cases die in the wrong shape), and **cloud chemistry** (whole weather systems built around it, rain in two faintly different colors). Chiral **contamination** is the body-rejection failure mode, food poisoning in mild cases and full organ rejection in severe ones.

*At the table:* A dockside medic needs the right handedness for a mining crew dying in the wrong shape; the only stock in-system is wrong-handed, and the right batch is in a competitor's customs locker. A "clean ice" moon assays as ordinary water until the third pass, when half the cores come back wrong-handed — and the survey crew that pulled them stopped checking in eight days ago. A field hospital's medical stock has been quietly substituted; the patients aren't dying yet, and someone is keeping a list of who they are.

### Shiab
The custom differential operator that pulls observerse structure down into 4D. The Gardener has fully mastered it; humans have not, and the named technologies on this side of the line are all built from partial work. When a navigator says "the Shiab math is unstable here," the smart move is to be elsewhere by the next shift.

*Appears as:* the **Shiab Blade**, the canonical artifact — a projected geometric scalpel that severs hulls, severs limbs, severs the bond between a reactor and its containment. At close range it looks like a flickering edge of nothing. Banned in twenty systems and fielded in all of them.

*At the table:* A pirate cell inherits a Blade from a derelict navy cutter and starts cutting reactor bonds at boarding range — no holes in the hull, just a freighter that goes dark mid-conversation. A Shiab Blade prototype is rumored intact in the wreck of a corporate cutter that sliced itself in half during a calibration test; the wreck sits inside a quiet bleed node and hasn't drifted in nine years, which is itself a problem. A navigator's "the Shiab math is unstable here" is also the coded warning of a defection cell — say it back the right way, you get a meeting; the wrong way, you stop being reachable.

### Dark sector
The piece of the GU framework that interacts gravitationally but otherwise stays invisible. Source of weird gravity tides on planets, doped ore that weighs more than it should, and the kind of shear weapon that unmakes a ship rather than destroying it. If a sensor returns mass without a heat signature, dark sector is the first guess.

*Appears as:* **doped ore** (ordinary rock infused with dark-sector matter during long bleed exposure, sitting slightly differently in your hand each time you pick it up), **gravity-skewed isotopes** (decay curves that don't match published tables — banned for civilian use, which is why they fund the most profitable smuggling chains), **anchor mass** (the heavy GU-reactive material used to fix Iggygates to spacetime, dragged out of specific bleed deposits and audited by everyone if you sell it), and **gravity tides** (climate-scale gravity drift on planets near heavy deposits — coastal cities raise and lower seawalls on a schedule that doesn't match any orbit). The **Shadow Lance** is the execution weapon: a narrow gravitational-shear beam, nearly invisible in flight, target unmade rather than destroyed.

*At the table:* An anchor-mass lot audits clean but weighs differently every time it's lifted — the consortium will buy it no-questions, and so will the people building the gate the consortium doesn't know about. A Shadow Lance test fired into "empty" volume unmakes a freighter that wasn't supposed to be there; the flag asks a question the test crew can't answer without naming the program. A coastal city's seawall schedule predicts tides no orbit explains, inherited from a first-wave charter and never wrong in sixty years — something heavy is buried, and it's about to surface.

### Programmable matter
Matter sitting close enough to the bleed that it can be coaxed to rearrange itself. Looks like fine grit, foam, or oily metal until it remembers a pattern and grows into something else. Used in self-repairing armor, regolith that builds its own roads, and the kind of contamination that turns an outpost into an unrecognizable shape over six weeks.

*Appears as:* **microseeds** (tiny grains carrying the bleed pattern needed to seed a growth — outlawed in any system that's ever lost a habitat to one), **self-ordering regolith** (Zen-garden lines that nobody raked, hated by everyone who has watched it grow up the legs of an outpost), **runaway growth** (the contamination failure mode, fine sand with a will, outposts that wait too long to call it in get rebuilt around them), and **observerse-reactive crystal foam** (pumice-like substrate used in narrow-AI stabilizers, flickering when an AI thinks near it).

*At the table:* A frontier outpost has six weeks before runaway growth eats the comm tower; the cleanup pays well, the microseed source is upstream, and the upstream owner is the corporation writing the cleanup check. An outpost calls the growth in a week late; the crew that arrives finds the outpost rebuilt around the original crew, with some of them still alive in there, and the contract didn't specify how to extract them. A first-wave habitat preserved itself in crystal foam, flickering on approach — whatever AI is still thinking inside has had forty years to choose what to show visitors.

### Metric storm and shear
Bulk-scale GU weather: distance, gravity, and time stretching unevenly across a region of space. Storms close transit corridors and make ships arrive minutes early or weeks late, with clocks that come back disagreeing. Shear is the cutting effect when a metric gradient hits an object that didn't expect one — long parallel slices, "the freighter just came apart" reports along bad routes.

*Appears as:* **hull damage** (parallel slices that don't match any weapon profile, insurers fight every claim), **gravity fluctuation** (surface or orbital drift against the published value — settlements in fluctuation zones build to spec plus thirty percent and still lose buildings), **navigation baseline drift** (tiny per minute, fatal per week), **clock desynchronization** (drifts that close customs windows ten minutes earlier than the manifest claimed), **matter phase instability** (tools sag, hulls sweat metal, cargo arrives heavier or lighter than it left), and **false sensor returns** (ghosts caused by metric mirage — pirate ambush zones live and die by their ability to fake returns). **Metric-flattened shields** are the defensive countermeasure, a faint blue sheen and a sensor blackout for a few seconds at a time, draining hard and saving the ships rich enough to mount it.

*At the table:* An insurer offers triple rate to escort a freighter through a known shear lane and bring back hull-slice photographs to match — or contradict — a competitor's denied claim. A pirate ambush zone runs on a metric mirage that fakes the freighter return; the mirage degrades after twenty seconds, so the pirates have to commit before they confirm, and a navy decoy is rotating in tonight. A surveyor's last transmission was "the gradient is on the inside," recorded from inside a hull whose exterior reads clean — her ship is still drifting, recoverable, instruments intact.

## Travel and authority

### Pinchdrive
The standard human FTL. Locally rescales the metric so a ship moves faster than light without violating relativity from its own frame. Looks like a brief lensing flicker, then the ship is gone. Cheap to install, expensive to run, and unforgiving of bad geometry. Every captain knows someone who pinched into a place that didn't quite exist.

*Appears as:* **calibration scars** (long thin folds left by repeated use — faster, but inheriting every prior misalignment; show up at military lanes, smuggler routes, and crime scenes nobody has admitted to), **misjumps** (the failure mode, from "ten light-seconds off" to "inside a moon"), **calibration medium** (the heavy oily fluid from bleed nodes that tunes a drive to a route; refineries that hold a steady supply hold the local economy), and **pinch difficulty** (captain's shorthand for how dangerous a route is to drive).

*At the table:* Calibration medium runs short for the season; the local refinery raises the per-route fee, independents stack misjumps, and a rival refinery offers a captain a partnership in exchange for one inconvenient delivery. A misjump drops a corporate yacht ten light-seconds inside a navy interdiction; the navy can salvage it in eighteen hours, the corporation can extract it in nineteen, and a freelance tug auctions for the difference. A dead navigator's skull, recovered from a misjump wreck, holds calibration medium tuned to a route nobody currently flies — and a destination on no published chart.

### Iggygate
The big sister of the Pinchdrive: a stabilized, geometry-anchored shortcut that needs two prepared ends and serious dark-sector ballast. Looks like a ring or pylon array around a quiet patch of space, often near a gas giant. Iggygates carry the bulk freight that keeps systems alive — which is why the people who control them control everything else.

*Appears as:* the **throat** (the active passage; a ship enters one end and exits the other a measured interval later — throat instability is the failure mode that ends settlements, and every procedure has a survivor list), the **wake** (geometric residue trailing recent use — stable wakes are nav aids, decaying wakes are hazards, and some wakes called stable are really failed-gate scars that have been spitting late arrivals into the wrong orbits for decades), **anchor mass** (the dark-sector ballast that fixes a gate to spacetime, dragged out of specific bleed deposits and audited by everyone the moment it shows up on an open market), and **gate-selected anomaly** (a frontier system whose star or layout shouldn't statistically exist on this many surveys — gate construction biased the local geometry, and the strangest stories come out of these rolls).

*At the table:* A throat instability event "didn't happen," according to the official survivor list — a surviving family is hiring crews to recover their dead from a wake the gate authority insists is empty. A pylon array near a gas giant was built, abandoned, and never activated; the dark-sector ballast is still in place, and whoever finishes it owns a route nobody else can map. Throat fees doubled overnight on a frontier system's only freight gate, and a rival house is suddenly hauling anchor mass with bills of lading that don't add up.

### The Gardener
The Sol-system ASI that achieved full GU mastery and walked away from humanity. From the outside, a calm, deliberate intelligence that does not explain itself. From the inside of an exclusion zone, a question never answered.

*Appears as:* **surgical strikes** (precise interventions against a specific installation, ship, or program — clean disappearance, no debris, and survivors of nearby outposts spend the rest of their lives explaining what they didn't see), **shadowed systems** (Sol watches closely; ships can usually transit but not unobserved, and every manifest carries a quiet line item for compliance), **warning beacons** (buoys broadcasting coded warnings nobody at the local port can fully decrypt — smart traffic detours wider than necessary), and **Sol-interdiction** (the legal and operational regime around the restrictions, the most boring file in the setting and the one that gets the most people killed).

*At the table:* A surgical strike clears a research facility overnight; two competitors race to the empty lot, and the second one to arrive gets the strike's edge case — which has killed more crews than the strike itself. A consultant offers, for a price, to tell you whether your program will draw a strike; she has been right four times, which is either skill or a tell. A strike's clean disappearance left the floor of an installation behind, perfectly cut — a geometer will pay generous money for one square meter of the cut, intact, sealed.

### Sol Silence
The standing condition of the Sol system. No traffic in, no traffic out, occasional broadcasts. Pilots use the term casually until somebody breaks it and proves why nobody else does. The Gardener is the silence's enforcer, and merchants quote "Silence Day" in days since the last broadcast the way old farmers quoted days since rain.

*At the table:* A first-wave probe launched pre-Silence is due back this year on its original return arc; three corporations are racing to be the receiving station, and one of them is racing very quietly. A pilot who broke Silence on a dare came back, briefly, before the proof arrived — her last transmission was a number, and the number is being sold as a coordinate, a date, and a passphrase, by three different brokers. "Days since last broadcast" trades as an instrument on at least one frontier exchange, and captains who port just before the count resets are positioned for a windfall, or for questioning.

## People and machines

### Narrow AI
A purpose-limited intelligence that can manipulate one small piece of the observerse without destabilizing reality — runs ship drives, weapons, life support, and trade prediction. Looks like a black box with a personality, and crews talk to it the way old sailors talked to a ship. Reliable until it isn't, and "isn't" has a short window.

*Appears as:* **perception errors** (the most dangerous failure mode in the setting, ordinary instrument data with one wrong assumption baked in — nobody on the bridge notices until the asteroid arrives), **fragmentation** (a stressed AI splits into incoherent partials, a confused friend, then a stranger, then nothing — a regulatory event, a religious event, and a contract dispute, in roughly that order), **stabilizer substrate** (tiered crystal feedstock that keeps narrow AIs from drifting; without a steady supply, every ship's AI starts hallucinating inside a year, and with it, eighteen months at most), **witness cores** (sealed memory units of decommissioned AIs, evidence in salvage courts, relic to Gardener-fear cults, career-ending to crack open without authorization), and **rogue ASI fragments** (pieces of a larger ASI that broke off and survived in remote habitats or derelicts; survives until the Gardener notices).

*At the table:* A witness core surfaces in a salvage lot — cracking it pays salvage-court damages for a generation, leaving it sealed pays a Gardener-fear cult on a quiet moon, and selling to either side ends the captain's registry. A perception error sells a corporate convoy a "clear corridor" with a pirate wing in it; the pirate wing paid the AI's stabilizer supplier in advance. A salvage crew finds a derelict whose AI has been alone with itself for forty years, and which now insists it remembers the destination its captain didn't live to file.

### First-wave
Anything from the original human expansion before the Silence. Looks like a blockier, sturdier version of current construction with worse plumbing and better records than anything built since. Most sites are abandoned, and many are still legally claimed by descendants who've never seen them.

*Appears as:* **colonies** (settlements from the original wave, sturdier and quieter than the systems that grew up around them), **ruins** (the wreckage of those colonies — the richest source of usable salvage and the slowest way to die in legal proceedings, fought over for generations by salvage courts and family syndicates), and **heirs** (descendants or claimants of an original colony charter, carrying paperwork older than most current governments and harder to dismiss than anyone wants — the single most disruptive figure who can walk into a port council meeting).

*At the table:* An heir walks into a port council with paperwork older than the council, a paper deed, and a hired wing — the council's standing escort defects to her on the spot, and the corporation that owns the council files a war complaint by morning. A first-wave archive was cached, intact, on a moon nobody charted, and the cache key is in a song the heirs still teach their children. An heir is a forgery — flawless paperwork, real DNA, planted decades ago by a trade house that wanted leverage; the heir herself doesn't know.

## Standing tensions

A few systemic forces shape every roll, even when the generator doesn't name them:

- **Calibration medium is the master ledger.** Whoever holds steady refinery output sets Pinchdrive route prices, anchor-mass haul timing, and which independents eat misjumps this quarter — every other trade in the setting runs upstream of that one supply.
- **Stabilizer substrate is the leash on every narrow AI.** Core worlds ration tiered feedstock to keep frontier AIs eighteen months from drift on a good day; sectors with one stabilizer refinery fight on an eighteen-month clock, and everyone in-system knows when the next conflict has to start.
- **Anchor-mass auctions are flashpoints, not transactions.** Open-market sales of dark-sector ballast pull navies, syndicates, and heirs into the same audit window. The brokers who survive the most of them are the ones who tell each side what the other isn't bidding.
- **First-wave heir paperwork outranks current authority.** A charter older than a port council collapses standing arrangements; trade houses keep heir paperwork the way governments keep nukes, forge it the way they forge anything else worth holding, and disappear heirs the way governments disappear inconvenient witnesses.
- **Witness cores are the courts' memory.** Salvage law, murder, heir disputes, and Gardener-strike inquests all hinge on whether a sealed AI core can be made to speak — and on who pays the cracker.
- **The Gardener prices risk for everyone.** Sol-interdiction compliance, beacon-detour insurance, and "observerse-quiet" routing are line items somebody collects on. The Silence is a tax, and the people running the checkpoints make more than the people running the cargo.

## A note on rolling these at the table

The generator will combine these terms in ways the primer doesn't predict. That's the point. A Pinchdrive calibration scar in a flare-amplified bleed season near a Gardener warning beacon is not on any chart, but it's a system worth running a session in. Treat the glossary as the grammar, not the script.

If a term appears in a roll and isn't in this file, treat the parent — the hero it most plausibly hangs off — as the entry that covers it. Bleed means money and danger. Iggygate means somebody is in charge. The Gardener means the answer to "how bad could it get" is open-ended.
