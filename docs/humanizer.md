# Optional Rule Humanizer: Writing Pattern Cleanup Guide

**Purpose:** This guide identifies and removes AI-generated writing patterns to keep Optional Rule content sounding like Scott Turnbull actually wrote it. It works alongside the Optional Rule Writing Style Guide, translating general AI-pattern awareness into rules specific to Scott's voice, rhythm, and editorial standards.

The goal is not just to strip out AI-isms. It's to make sure every piece reads like a hobbyist and game designer sharing real thoughts and experience, not a language model assembling plausible paragraphs about TTRPGs.

---

## The Optional Rule Voice Test

Before diving into specific patterns, here's the quick gut check. Read the sentence out loud. If it sounds like something you'd find in a press release or a corporate product description, rewrite it. If it sounds like something Scott would say at the game table while explaining why a rule works or doesn't, it's probably fine.

Optional Rule writing is:

- First-person and conversational, like talking directly to a fellow gamer
- Grounded in actual play experience and design thinking, not abstracted into platitudes
- Enthusiastic without being breathless
- Willing to have a take and defend it, while still being fair-minded
- Self-aware enough to crack a joke at its own expense once or twice

Optional Rule writing is NOT:

- Marketing copy for a game publisher
- Neutral to the point of having no perspective
- Academic or lecture-like
- Structured like an outline pretending to be prose
- A Wikipedia summary of game mechanics

---

## Hard Rules (Non-Negotiable)

These always apply, regardless of context. They come directly from the Optional Rule Writing Style Guide and override any default behavior.

1. **No em dashes.** Ever. Rewrite the sentence using commas, periods, parentheses, or restructuring. If you find one, kill it.
2. **No colons in narrative openings.** Don't start a thought with "Here's the thing:" or "The answer is simple:" Just say it.
3. **No bullet points or numbered lists** unless Scott explicitly asks for them or they already exist in a draft being edited. Prefer narrative flow.
4. **No AI cliché phrases.** "Rich tapestry of storytelling," "immersive experience," "collaborative narrative," "at the intersection of," "the evolving landscape" are all banned unless deliberately subverted.
5. **No ellipses.** Period.
6. **American English** spelling and grammar throughout.
7. **No emoji** in published content.

## Voice and Soul

Avoiding AI patterns is only half the work. Clean but lifeless writing is just as obvious as slop. Optional Rule content needs a human behind it.

### Signs the writing is technically clean but still dead:

- Every sentence is roughly the same length
- No first-person perspective or personal reaction
- Opinions are hedged into nothingness
- The piece reads like a Wikipedia summary of game mechanics
- There's no sense that the writer has actually played the games or run the sessions they're describing

### How to fix it:

**Have a take.** Scott's writing doesn't just report on game mechanics. It reacts, reflects, and occasionally pushes back. "Focusing on character agency decreases the need for player agency. This is a bad thing for games." That's a person thinking on the page.

**Vary the rhythm.** Short declarative sentences next to longer reflective ones. This is one of the strongest markers of Scott's style. "A class is a class is a class." Then a beat of elaboration. Then another short punch.

**Acknowledge complexity without waffling.** Real humans have mixed feelings. "This isn't to say character-focused games are bad, but when you place everything in automatically obtained character abilities, you remove the need for the player to engage with the world" is honest tension, not hedging.

**Use "I" naturally.** First person isn't unprofessional. It's how hobbyists and designers talk. "I mostly built it for myself" carries more authenticity than "The tool was developed to address a gap in available resources."

**Be specific.** Not "this changes the game experience" but "it means a 5th-level fighter with a Flame Tongue and a Ring of Protection plays completely differently from a 5th-level fighter without them."

**Use natural transitions.** Scott's writing uses casual connectors like "So," "Anyway," "Still though," "Let me explain." Avoid stiff academic transitions like "Furthermore," "Therefore," "Additionally."

**Allow self-deprecating humor.** A light touch goes a long way. "In the tradition of creating content nobody cares about" and "Or am I just a grognard romanticizing tedious bookkeeping?" are authentic to Scott's voice.

### Before (clean but soulless):
> The advancement system in modern TTRPGs differs significantly from earlier editions. Character progression has become more streamlined, with abilities primarily tied to class features. This represents a shift in design philosophy that affects player engagement with game systems.

### After (has a pulse):
> Modern TTRPGs bundle everything into the character and their abilities. In D&D, this specifically means class abilities, but the entire shift toward narrative and character-focused games has led to this design principle being the norm. So why bother with things like foraging or crafting items? Why spend any game time on it, and what tension could it possibly create?

## Content Patterns to Catch and Fix

### 1. Inflated significance

**Watch for:** "stands as," "serves as a testament," "a pivotal moment," "underscores the importance," "reflects broader trends," "setting the stage for," "indelible mark"

These phrases puff up ordinary statements into fake profundity. Optional Rule writing earns its weight through specifics, not inflation.

**Before:**
> This house rule serves as a testament to the growing recognition of the importance of player agency in modern TTRPG design, underscoring a pivotal shift in how we think about game mechanics.

**After:**
> I started using this house rule because my players were bored during combat. It fixed the problem, and now I can't imagine running a game without it.

### 2. Vague authority claims

**Watch for:** "Experts agree," "Many GMs have found," "The community has long recognized," "Studies suggest"

If you can't name the source or cite the specific discussion, don't gesture at its existence. Scott's writing supports claims with personal experience, specific examples, or references to actual games and designers.

**Before:**
> Many experienced game masters agree that encounter balance is one of the most challenging aspects of running a successful campaign.

**After:**
> I've been running games for years and I still can't reliably balance an encounter. The CR system in 5e is partly to blame, but honestly it's more that player creativity makes prediction nearly impossible.

### 3. Participle padding (-ing phrases)

**Watch for:** "highlighting the need for," "ensuring balanced gameplay," "reflecting a broader shift toward," "showcasing the potential of," "fostering creativity"

These tack shallow analysis onto the end of sentences. They feel like the writer ran out of actual things to say.

**Before:**
> The new edition introduced a revised skill system last year, highlighting the growing need for streamlined mechanics and showcasing its commitment to accessible game design.

**After:**
> The new edition introduced a revised skill system last year. It cuts the skill list in half and ties everything to the six ability scores. Whether that's an improvement depends on what you want from your game.

### 4. Promotional language

**Watch for:** "groundbreaking," "vibrant," "rich," "renowned," "stunning," "boasts," "nestled," "must-read," "profound impact," "game-changing" (unless literally about games)

Optional Rule is a hobby blog, not marketing copy. The voice is enthusiastic and clear, not breathless. Let the actual content do the work.

**Before:**
> This groundbreaking supplement offers a vibrant collection of stunning new subclasses that will have a profound impact on your campaigns, boasting a rich array of innovative mechanics.

**After:**
> The supplement has six new subclasses, and two of them are genuinely interesting. The Storm Herald rework gives martial characters something to do with their bonus action, which is worth the price of admission by itself.

### 5. The formulaic "challenges and future" section

**Watch for:** "Despite these challenges," "Looking ahead," "The future looks bright," "As the hobby continues to evolve"

AI-generated articles almost always end with a vague optimistic outlook bolted onto a list of challenges. Scott's writing closes with genuine reflection, a question for readers, or a self-aware joke. Not a shrug toward the horizon.

**Before:**
> Despite these challenges, the future of tabletop roleplaying looks bright. As the hobby continues to evolve, those who embrace innovative approaches to game design will be well-positioned to create memorable experiences.

**After:**
> Maybe that's why KCD2's alchemy system resonates with me so much. It requires actual player engagement. It reminds me of what we've lost in the shift to streamlined TTRPG design. Sure, modern games are more accessible, but sometimes I miss needing to actually work for my character's abilities instead of just leveling up. What are your thoughts? Or am I just a grognard romanticizing tedious bookkeeping?

## Language Patterns to Catch and Fix

### 6. Overused AI vocabulary

**Banned or flagged words (use sparingly or not at all):**

Additionally, align with, captivating, collaborative storytelling, crucial, curate, delve, dynamic, elevate, emphasize, enduring, enhance, epic (as generic praise), foster, garner, highlight (as verb), immersive, interplay, intricate/intricacies, key (as adjective), landscape (abstract), leverage (as verb), multifaceted, navigate (abstract), pivotal, realm (abstract), robust, seamless, showcase, synergy, tapestry (abstract), testament, transformative, underscore, unlock, valuable, vibrant, weave (abstract)

**Optional Rule preferred alternatives:** Use plain English. "Important" instead of "crucial." "Show" instead of "showcase." "Help" instead of "foster." "Complicated" instead of "multifaceted." "Fun" instead of "engaging experience." If the simpler word works, use it.

### 7. Copula avoidance (ducking "is" and "are")

**Watch for:** "serves as," "stands as," "represents," "boasts," "features," "offers"

AI models avoid simple "is/are" constructions. Scott's writing doesn't. Simple is fine.

**Before:**
> The gritty rest variant serves as a comprehensive alternative for groups seeking to implement a more realistic survival experience in their campaigns.

**After:**
> The gritty rest variant is a longer rest cycle that makes healing scarce and travel dangerous. It's great for survival games.

### 8. Negative parallelism

**Watch for:** "It's not just about X, it's about Y," "Not only...but also," "It's not merely...it's..."

These constructions are AI comfort food. They sound punchy but say almost nothing. Rewrite as a direct statement.

**Before:**
> It's not just about rolling dice, it's about creating shared narratives. Not only does this enhance gameplay, but it also deepens player investment.

**After:**
> Rolling dice is fun, but the real engagement comes from players making choices that matter. When those choices have real consequences, players pay attention.

### 9. Rule of three overuse

AI forces ideas into triads to seem comprehensive. Use groups of three only when you genuinely have three distinct points. Two is fine. Four is fine. Don't manufacture the third item.

**Before:**
> Great encounter design requires tension, creativity, and a commitment to player engagement.

**After:**
> Great encounter design requires tension and a reason for players to care about the outcome.

### 10. Synonym cycling

AI has repetition penalties that cause excessive synonym substitution. Scott's writing repeats key terms naturally rather than cycling through alternatives that muddy meaning.

**Before:**
> The game master designed an encounter. The referee then adjusted the challenge. The storyteller presented the scenario to the group.

**After:**
> The GM designed the encounter, adjusted the difficulty after the first round, and let the players figure out the rest.

## Style Patterns to Catch and Fix

### 11. Em dashes (critical for Optional Rule)

This gets its own section because it's the single most common AI writing artifact and it's a hard rule in Optional Rule style. Every em dash (—) must be removed and the sentence restructured.

**Before:**
> The monster has three attacks—a bite, a claw, and a tail swipe—each dealing different damage types.

**After:**
> The monster has three attacks (a bite, a claw, and a tail swipe), each dealing different damage types.

**Before:**
> Player agency isn't optional—it's the whole point.

**After:**
> Player agency isn't optional. It's the whole point.

### 12. Overuse of bold text

AI chatbots mechanically bold key phrases. Optional Rule uses bold sparingly and only for genuine structural emphasis in longer pieces, not for every game term or concept name.

**Before:**
> Players should track their **hit points**, manage their **spell slots**, and monitor their **exhaustion levels** throughout the session.

**After:**
> Players should track their hit points, manage their spell slots, and monitor their exhaustion levels throughout the session.

### 13. Inline-header vertical lists

AI loves to produce lists where each item starts with a bolded keyword followed by a colon. Optional Rule writing avoids lists entirely unless explicitly requested. Convert these to narrative.

**Before:**
> - **Preparation:** Review your notes before the session.
> - **Flexibility:** Be ready to improvise when players go off-script.
> - **Pacing:** Keep combat encounters moving quickly.

**After:**
> Review your notes before the session, but don't over-prepare. Players will go off-script, and that's fine. The bigger concern is pacing. If combat drags, the whole session suffers.

### 14. Title case in headings

Optional Rule uses sentence case for subheadings. Only the first word and proper nouns get capitalized.

**Before:**
> ## Building A Better Encounter For Your D&D Campaign

**After:**
> ## Building a better encounter for your D&D campaign

## TTRPG-Specific AI Patterns to Strip

### 15. Generic TTRPG filler

**Watch for these hollow phrases that AI loves to insert into gaming content:**

- "Around the table" (used as filler, not when literally relevant)
- "Shared storytelling experience"
- "Memorable moments"
- "Collaborative narrative"
- "Epic adventures"
- "Immersive world-building"
- "A rich tapestry of lore"
- "Bring your world to life"
- "Take your game to the next level"
- "Whether you're a seasoned veteran or a new player"

These are the TTRPG equivalent of "synergy" and "leverage." They fill space without saying anything. Replace with specific observations or cut them entirely.

### 16. Chatbot residue

**Watch for:** "I hope this helps," "Of course!", "Certainly!", "Let me know if you'd like me to expand," "Here is a," "Would you like," "Great question!"

These are conversational artifacts from AI chat interfaces. They should never appear in published content.

### 17. Hedging and filler

**Cut these phrases down:**

- "In order to" becomes "To"
- "Due to the fact that" becomes "Because"
- "It is important to note that" becomes just state the thing
- "At this point in time" becomes "Now"
- "Has the ability to" becomes "Can"
- "It could potentially be argued that" becomes "Some argue" or just make the argument
- "When it comes to" becomes cut it and start with the subject

### 18. Generic positive conclusions

AI defaults to vague optimism at the end of pieces. Optional Rule articles close with genuine reflection, a question to readers, or a light self-deprecating joke. Not sunshine.

**Before:**
> The future of tabletop gaming is bright, and by embracing these techniques, you'll create unforgettable experiences for everyone at your table.

**After:**
> I'd love to hear how you handle this at your table. Have you found a system that makes crafting actually fun? Or am I chasing something that only ever existed in my imagination? Let me know what you think.

## The Revision Checklist

Before finalizing any piece, run through this:

1. **Em dashes?** Remove every single one.
2. **Colons opening narrative sentences?** Rewrite them.
3. **Bullet points or numbered lists?** Convert to prose unless explicitly requested.
4. **AI vocabulary?** (crucial, delve, landscape, foster, showcase, underscore, immersive, elevate, etc.) Replace with plain English.
5. **Inflated significance?** ("serves as a testament," "pivotal role," "reflects broader trends") Cut or rewrite with specifics.
6. **Vague attributions?** ("Many GMs agree," "The community recognizes") Cite a source, use personal experience, or cut the claim.
7. **Participle padding?** ("highlighting," "showcasing," "ensuring") Remove and state the point directly.
8. **Promotional language?** ("groundbreaking," "vibrant," "renowned") Replace with concrete observations.
9. **Negative parallelisms?** ("It's not just...it's...") Rewrite as direct statements.
10. **Rule-of-three forcing?** Only group in threes if you actually have three distinct points.
11. **Bold overuse?** Strip unnecessary bold formatting.
12. **Sentence rhythm?** Mix short punchy sentences with longer reflective ones.
13. **First person?** Use it. This is Scott writing, not a committee.
14. **Natural transitions?** Use "So," "Anyway," "Still though," "Let me explain." Not "Furthermore," "Therefore," "Additionally."
15. **Does it have a take?** If the piece could have been written by anyone about anything, it needs more Scott.
16. **TTRPG filler?** ("shared storytelling experience," "around the table," "memorable moments") Cut or replace with something specific.
17. **Closing?** Ends with genuine reflection, a question for readers, or a light joke. Not vague optimism about the future of gaming.
